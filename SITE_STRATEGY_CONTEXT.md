# Site Strategy Context

## Purpose of this document

This document stores the evolving strategic context for rethinking the website. It is the single working source for:

- site concept
- positioning
- information architecture
- homepage logic
- user entry paths
- solutions catalog
- future decisions from next prompts

It should be updated as the project discussion continues so context is not lost between iterations.

---

## 1. Strategic direction

### Core context

The site is for an AI developer who builds websites, bots, applications, AI agents, and automations using artificial intelligence and web development.

### Main problem

Most clients do not understand the difference between a website, bot, application, AI agent, and automation. When they arrive on the site, it is unclear:

- what these things are
- whether they need them
- which one to choose

### New site task

The site should not simply display services. It should help the client:

1. understand what kinds of digital solutions exist
2. see the difference between them
3. understand what fits their situation
4. go through a mini-diagnostic
5. see examples and cases
6. understand how the work happens and what they are paying for

### Desired qualities of the website

The website should feel:

- clear
- modern
- professional
- transparent
- not overloaded

It should sell through clarity, not pressure.

---

## 2. New concept of the website

### Main concept

The website should be rethought not as a portfolio of an AI developer, but as a clear platform for choosing digital solutions for business.

### Short formulation

Not a showcase of services, but a navigation system for choosing the right solution.

### Expanded formulation

The site should answer not only "what can I build?" but the client question:

"What do I actually need: a website, a bot, an application, an AI agent, or automation?"

### Working positioning statement

Platform for choosing digital solutions for business, where complex technologies are translated into clear business tasks, scenarios, and outcomes.

### Strategic formula

The website is a guide that turns client confusion into a clear choice of digital solution.

---

## 3. Main role of the website

The site should act as a digital consultant, not just a portfolio.

### Its main role

Help the client make the right decision without technical confusion.

### Key functions

1. Explain
   Show what kinds of solutions exist and why each one is needed.

2. Diagnose
   Help the visitor match their task to the right solution format.

3. Reduce anxiety
   Remove the feeling of "I do not understand this, so I cannot choose."

4. Prove competence
   Through logic, examples, cases, and a transparent process.

5. Lead to the next step
   Not "buy now", but "let's determine what suits your task."

---

## 4. How it differs from a typical portfolio

### Typical portfolio logic

A normal portfolio usually says:

- here are my projects
- here are my skills
- here are my services
- contact me

### New website logic

This site should say:

- here are the kinds of business tasks people have
- here are the digital formats that solve them
- here is how those formats differ
- here is how to understand what fits you
- here is how I design and implement the right solution

### Core difference

A normal portfolio is centered on the performer.  
This site should be centered on the client’s choice.

### Positioning shift

From:

"I build websites, bots, apps, and AI."

To:

"I help businesses choose and implement the right digital solution for their task."

## 5. What the visitor should understand in the first 10 seconds

The visitor should quickly understand four things:

1. They will be helped to figure out what they need.
2. Website, bot, application, AI agent, and automation are different tools for different situations.
3. They do not need to understand the technology themselves.
4. The specialist is not just a developer, but someone who helps design the right solution for a business goal.

### Core meanings the site must communicate

- I do not sell abstract AI. I help choose the right tool for the business.
- Different tasks require different solutions.
- A strong result starts with the right diagnosis, not just development.
- Technology can be explained clearly and transparently.
- The client pays not only for code, but for logic, design of the solution, implementation, and fewer mistakes.

## 7. Homepage structure

The homepage should not try to explain everything at once. It should act as a route selector.

### Homepage sections

1. Hero section
   Main message: the site helps a client understand what digital solution they need and then implement it.

2. Short explanation block
   Explain that many people confuse websites, bots, applications, AI agents, and automations, and this site helps them understand the difference.

3. Three user entry paths
   - I do not understand what I need
   - I want to explore solutions
   - I want to see cases and understand how you work

4. Solutions overview
   Quick cards for the five main solution types.

5. Simple comparison block
   Show when each type is usually appropriate.

6. Diagnostics invitation
   Encourage the visitor to answer a few questions and get a recommendation.

7. Featured cases
   Show strong examples with the logic of why a certain solution was chosen.

8. How I work
   A short 4 to 5 step process.

9. Transparency / terms
   Explain what the client pays for and how the work is structured.

10. FAQ
   Answer core doubts.

11. Final CTA
   Calm next steps: diagnostics, discuss a task, explore solutions.

## 9. Purposes of the main pages

### Home

Explains the idea of the site, routes the visitor through three scenarios, previews solutions, and offers the next step.

### Solutions

A catalog of solution formats explained in plain language: what they are, who they fit, when to choose them, and examples.

Explains one format in more detail: its purpose, scenarios, limits, development logic, case examples, and possible budget/time guidance.

An educational page for a non-technical client. It helps compare formats and avoid a wrong choice.

### Diagnostics

A mini-quiz or guided decision flow that translates a vague task into a clear direction.

### Cases / Portfolio

Shows real examples with business logic, not just visuals or technology lists.

### How I work

Explains the process from task analysis to launch and improvement.

Explains payment logic, stages, scope changes, support, and responsibility clearly.

### Blog / Help

Educational content that helps the client understand the topic and build trust before contacting.

### FAQ

Fast answers to repeated questions and objections.

### Contact / Request

Simple entry point to describe a task and start a conversation.

## 10. Homepage block based on client tasks

### Purpose of the block

This block should help the visitor choose by need, not by technical terminology.

### Suggested block titles

- What do you need to solve?
- What can I help with?

### Suggested intro idea

If you do not know whether you need a website, bot, application, AI agent, or automation, start with your task.

### Client tasks for cards

#### 1. Get more leads

Description:
If you have a service or product but your current site does not bring clients, or you do not have a clear entry point at all, you need a place that explains the offer and leads a person to a request.

Best-fit solutions:

- website
- bot
- automation

Short card angle:
Create a clear website or funnel that helps visitors understand the offer and leave a request. If needed, add a bot and automated lead handling.

#### 2. Clearly present services

Description:
If clients do not fully understand what you offer, how you differ, or why they should contact you, the problem may be packaging and clarity, not traffic.

Best-fit solutions:

- website
- application

Short card angle:
Turn a complex or scattered offer into a clear digital presentation with structure, logic, and pages that make the value obvious.

#### 3. Automate communication

Description:
If you or your managers spend too much time answering the same questions, clarifying requests, and qualifying leads, this can be simplified.

Best-fit solutions:

- bot
- AI agent
- automation

Short card angle:
Set up a bot or AI flow that answers common questions, gathers data, qualifies requests, and forwards only the cases that need human attention.

#### 4. Simplify booking

Description:
If booking happens through messages, spreadsheets, and manual coordination, clients can get confused, wait too long, or leave.

Best-fit solutions:

- website
- bot
- application
- automation

Short card angle:
Build a simpler booking flow through a site, bot, or service so the client can choose a time and you do not have to coordinate everything manually.

#### 5. Deliver materials or access automatically

Description:
If after payment, request, or signup you manually send files, links, instructions, or access rights, this can be automated.

Best-fit solutions:

- bot
- application
- automation
- AI agent

Short card angle:
Set up a system that automatically sends the right materials, instructions, or access at the right moment without manual work every time.

#### 6. Create a convenient client service

Description:
If clients need a personal account, order flow, tracking, or another digital self-service experience, a simple website may not be enough.

Best-fit solutions:

- application
- website
- AI agent

Short card angle:
Create a service where clients can access information, perform actions, and solve their tasks online in a more convenient way.

#### 7. Reduce manual routine

Description:
If the team spends too much time copying data, forwarding information, updating spreadsheets, and repeating the same actions, growth becomes harder.

Best-fit solutions:

- automation
- AI agent
- application

Short card angle:
Automate repetitive processes so less time goes to routine and more time goes to valuable work.

#### 8. Launch a digital solution without chaos

Description:
If you have an idea but do not know where to start, what should come first, and what format is actually needed, you need a structured path, not just development.

Best-fit solutions:

- website
- bot
- application
- AI agent
- automation

Short card angle:
Clarify the task, choose the right format, and launch the solution step by step without unnecessary complexity.

## 12. Solution category content

### Websites

#### Simple explanation

A website helps present your business, service, or product online in a clear way and lead a visitor to the next action: request, purchase, booking, or contact.

#### Who it fits

Fits those who need to:

- attract new clients
- clearly show services
- collect requests
- create trust
- have a clear destination for traffic, search, and recommendations

#### What task it solves

Explains who you are, what you offer, who it is for, how it works, and what the client should do next.

#### When it does not fit

Not the best choice if:

- you need ongoing interaction, not just information and conversion
- the task is mostly conversation-based
- you need an internal working tool
- the main problem is process automation rather than presentation

#### Examples

- service website
- landing page
- portfolio website
- consultation booking website
- product presentation website
- multi-page company website

#### Minimal starting format

- one-page website
- landing page for one offer
- compact website with 3 to 5 key sections
- clear page with value proposition and a request form

#### How it can scale later

- more pages and directions
- CRM and analytics integrations
- booking and forms
- connection to bots and automations
- personal account or transition into a more functional service

### Bots

#### Simple explanation

A bot helps communicate with a client automatically: answer common questions, collect requests, guide people step by step, share information, or help with booking.

#### Who it fits

Fits those who have:

- many repetitive questions
- leads coming through messengers
- need for a fast first contact
- a need to simplify consultation, booking, or information delivery

#### What task it solves

Reduces manual communication and guides people through a simple scenario: identify a need, gather data, answer, book, or send materials.

#### When it does not fit

Not the best choice if:

- you need deep presentation of the company and services
- visitors need to study information in depth before deciding
- you need a complex interface with many functions
- there is no clear communication scenario yet

#### Examples

- booking bot
- initial consultation bot
- bot for guides or material delivery
- lead collection bot
- support bot for frequent questions

#### Minimal starting format

- bot with 3 to 5 main scenarios
- bot for requests and FAQ
- bot for booking or service navigation
- bot that collects contacts and passes the request onward

#### How it can scale later

- AI-based flexible answers
- integration with CRM, spreadsheets, and calendars
- lead segmentation
- automatic qualification
- integration with website, email, and internal processes

### Applications

#### Simple explanation

An application is a more functional digital tool where the user does not just read information, but performs actions: manages data, places orders, uses a personal account, or works with ongoing processes.

#### Who it fits

Fits those who need to:

- build a service for clients
- provide a personal account
- organize an internal tool for a team
- create a convenient interface for regular use
- combine several functions in one system

#### What task it solves

Solves ongoing interaction tasks where a client or employee needs to repeatedly do something inside a system, not just consume information.

#### When it does not fit

Not the best choice if:

- a simple website or bot is enough
- you only need to test demand
- the usage scenario is not clear yet
- users do not need ongoing interaction

#### Examples

- personal account
- booking and service management system
- internal team tool
- order service
- platform with access to materials and functions

#### Minimal starting format

- MVP with one core function
- personal account with basic access
- simple internal tool for one task
- web application without unnecessary secondary features

#### How it can scale later

- new user roles
- more functions
- payments, notifications, analytics
- mobile version
- integration with AI, bots, and automations

### AI agents

#### Simple explanation

An AI agent is a solution that helps handle tasks in a smarter way: understand requests, work with context, find relevant information, prepare actions, or assist in decision-making.

#### Who it fits

Fits those who have:

- repeated intellectual tasks
- a need to process requests, texts, data, or questions
- a desire to reduce manual cognitive work
- a large knowledge base that should be easier to use

#### What task it solves

Useful when regular automation is not enough because requests are not always identical and the system needs to interpret context.

#### When it does not fit

Not the best choice if:

- the task is simple and linear
- a regular bot or automation is enough
- there is no clear information source or data for it to use
- you need a fully rigid scenario without variability

#### Examples

- AI consultant for services
- AI assistant for incoming leads
- knowledge base assistant
- helper for preparing replies, summaries, or drafts
- AI support interface for internal teams

#### Minimal starting format

- AI consultant for one task
- agent based on your knowledge base
- AI helper for first-line client communication
- narrow scenario with immediate time savings

#### How it can scale later

- more data sources
- more scenarios
- task handoff between AI and team
- integration into site, app, bot, and internal workflows
- more complex multi-step logic

### Automations

#### Simple explanation

Automation removes manual actions between services, forms, spreadsheets, messages, and internal processes. It connects repetitive work so it happens more smoothly.

#### Who it fits

Fits those who have:

- repeated actions
- manual transfer of requests
- duplicated data
- too much time wasted on routine
- processes that depend too much on human control

#### What task it solves

Reduces routine, lowers errors, speeds up work, and supports growth without increasing manual workload in the same proportion.

#### When it does not fit

Not the best choice if:

- the process itself is not clear yet
- the business is still chaotic and nothing stable exists to automate
- the task is one-time rather than repeated
- the core problem is positioning or packaging, not process work

#### Examples

- automatic lead transfer into CRM
- notifications and emails
- delivery of materials after payment or signup
- automatic task creation for managers
- syncing data between forms, tables, and services

#### Minimal starting format

- automate 1 to 2 painful routine processes
- move leads without manual copying
- automatic notifications and reminders
- basic connection between website, form, spreadsheet, and messenger

#### How it can scale later

- unite several processes into one system
- add conditional logic
- add AI-based processing
- automate parts of sales, service, and operations
- build near-autonomous workflow chains

## 14. Detailed page structure: "Websites"

### Role of the page

The "Websites" page should help a non-technical client understand:

- what a website is in practical terms
- why a business may need one
- which website format fits which situation
- how website types differ from one another
- how to avoid choosing the wrong format too early

This page should not feel like a technical classification. It should feel like a practical guide for choosing the right website format.

### Core idea of the page

A website is not just "a presence on the internet."  
It is a digital point of contact that can explain, attract, qualify, convert, support trust, and sometimes even provide a service.

### What a website is

A website is a digital space where a client gets acquainted with your business, understands your offer, and moves to the next step:

- leave a request
- book a service
- contact you
- study your offer
- choose a format of cooperation
- use a tool or service

### Why a website is needed

A website is useful when you need to:

- clearly present yourself or your business
- explain services or products
- create trust
- collect requests
- support advertising or traffic
- organize information in one clear place
- give people an easy next step

### Main message for the page

Different website formats solve different tasks.  
It is not about choosing "a website" in general, but choosing the right website format for your business stage and goal.

### Website formats

#### 1. Website business card

##### Simple explanation

A business-card website is a compact website that introduces you, your service, or your company in a simple and professional way.

##### Who it fits

Fits:

- private specialists
- local businesses
- consultants
- small service companies
- anyone who needs a clean online presence without a large structure

##### What tasks it solves

- helps people quickly understand who you are
- creates trust
- shows key services
- gives contacts and a clear way to reach out
- replaces scattered information from messengers and social media

##### When it is better not to choose it

Not the best choice if:

- you have many services that need explanation
- you need traffic from search across multiple topics
- you need complex lead funnels
- you plan to regularly expand content and sections

##### Life scenario example

A psychologist, lawyer, designer, or local studio needs one clean website where a person can quickly understand the offer, see credibility, and leave a request.

##### How it differs from other formats

It is simpler and lighter than a multi-page website.  
It is more stable and representative than a single landing page built only for one offer.  
It is not meant for complex structure or deep user interaction.

#### 2. Landing page

##### Simple explanation

A landing page is a focused one-page website built around one offer, one audience, and one main action.

##### Who it fits

Fits:

- businesses launching one product or service
- ad traffic campaigns
- event, course, or offer launches
- those who need a clear conversion page without extra distractions

##### What tasks it solves

- presents one offer clearly
- leads a visitor to one target action
- improves conversion from ads or targeted traffic
- quickly tests demand for a product or service

##### When it is better not to choose it

Not the best choice if:

- you need to explain many services
- your business has multiple audiences
- you need a long-term structured website
- your goal is broad company presentation rather than one offer conversion

##### Life scenario example

A specialist launches a new consultation package or a company promotes one flagship service through ads and needs a focused page that converts traffic into requests.

##### How it differs from other formats

It is more conversion-focused than a business-card website.  
It is narrower than a multi-page site.  
It is usually built around one offer, not around the whole business.

#### 3. Multi-page website

##### Simple explanation

A multi-page website is a structured site with several sections or pages that explains a business, its services, approach, cases, and contact options in more detail.

##### Who it fits

Fits:

- companies with several services
- experts with a broader offer
- businesses that need a stronger presentation
- those who want a more complete online system, not just one page

##### What tasks it solves

- explains different services clearly
- helps different types of visitors find relevant information
- builds trust through depth and structure
- supports search visibility and long-term growth
- gives room for cases, FAQ, process, and supporting pages

##### When it is better not to choose it

Not the best choice if:

- you only need to launch one narrow offer quickly
- your business is still too early to justify many sections
- the offer is not yet clear enough for a larger structure

##### Life scenario example

A small studio or agency offers several directions and needs separate pages for services, cases, process, and contact so clients can understand the business properly.

##### How it differs from other formats

It is broader and more flexible than a landing page or business-card site.  
It is better for businesses with multiple directions.  
It is less product-like than a web app and more presentation-focused.

#### 4. Catalog website

##### Simple explanation

A catalog website helps present many products, services, or items in a structured way so people can browse, compare, and choose more easily.

##### Who it fits

Fits:

- businesses with a range of products
- service companies with many directions or packages
- showrooms, suppliers, studios, and niche stores
- those who need structured browsing before a request or order

##### What tasks it solves

- organizes many offers in one place
- helps clients navigate options
- shows categories, items, and details clearly
- reduces confusion when the offer is too large for a regular website page

##### When it is better not to choose it

Not the best choice if:

- you only have one or two services
- your offer is simple and does not need a catalog structure
- the main goal is brand presentation rather than browsing options

##### Life scenario example

A furniture studio, equipment supplier, or clinic with many services needs clients to browse categories and understand options before making contact.

##### How it differs from other formats

It is more structured than a standard multi-page site when there are many positions or categories.  
It focuses on navigation and selection, not only presentation.  
It is still simpler than a full web service.

#### 5. Portfolio website

##### Simple explanation

A portfolio website shows your work, approach, style, and results so a client can understand what you do and whether they trust your level.

##### Who it fits

Fits:

- designers
- developers
- creators
- studios
- consultants and experts whose credibility depends on previous work

##### What tasks it solves

- demonstrates experience visually and structurally
- builds trust through examples
- helps a visitor understand your style or approach
- supports personal positioning and professional image

##### When it is better not to choose it

Not the best choice if:

- you do not yet have enough material to show
- the business depends more on process or utility than on showcased work
- you actually need a lead-generation site with clearer service packaging

##### Life scenario example

A photographer, UX designer, developer, or strategist needs a website where clients can review projects, understand the level of work, and then decide whether to reach out.

##### How it differs from other formats

It puts proof and examples in the center.  
Unlike a landing page, it is not only built around one offer.  
Unlike a business-card website, it usually gives more space to cases and work samples.

#### 6. Website with quiz or diagnostics

##### Simple explanation

This is a website that not only presents an offer, but also helps a visitor go through a short diagnostic, answer questions, and get a more relevant recommendation.

##### Who it fits

Fits:

- experts with complex services
- businesses where clients are confused about what to choose
- projects with several solution paths
- those who want to turn uncertainty into a guided next step

##### What tasks it solves

- helps the user self-identify their need
- simplifies choice
- increases engagement
- gathers useful information before contact
- makes the site feel more consultative and helpful

##### When it is better not to choose it

Not the best choice if:

- the offer is extremely simple and obvious
- visitors do not need guidance to choose
- you are not ready to think through the recommendation logic

##### Life scenario example

A specialist offers several formats of help, and clients often do not know what suits them. A short quiz helps them choose a direction and then leave a more informed request.

##### How it differs from other formats

It is more interactive than a standard landing page or multi-page site.  
Its role is not only to present, but also to guide and qualify.  
It works especially well when the client needs help making sense of options.

#### 7. Personal brand website

##### Simple explanation

A personal brand website is built around a person: their expertise, ideas, style, experience, and trust. It helps people understand not only what you do, but why they should work with you specifically.

##### Who it fits

Fits:

- consultants
- experts
- speakers
- creators
- founders
- specialists whose reputation is closely tied to their name

##### What tasks it solves

- builds personal credibility
- presents expertise in a more human way
- combines services, content, biography, and cases
- creates a stronger connection before contact

##### When it is better not to choose it

Not the best choice if:

- the business should be centered on the company, not the person
- you do not want the communication to depend on your personal public role
- your main need is a fast conversion page for one narrow offer

##### Life scenario example

A consultant, AI specialist, coach, or strategist wants a website where people can understand their philosophy, experience, services, and cases in one coherent space.

##### How it differs from other formats

It is more identity-driven than a regular business website.  
It centers trust in the person, not only the service.  
It often combines portfolio, expertise, offer, and thought leadership.

#### 8. Web app / mini-service

##### Simple explanation

A web app or mini-service is a website-like digital product where the user does not just read information, but performs actions: fills in data, gets a result, uses a tool, manages a process, or interacts with a service.

##### Who it fits

Fits:

- businesses that need a client tool
- internal team workflows
- service platforms
- projects where utility matters more than presentation alone
- founders testing a digital product idea

##### What tasks it solves

- gives the user practical functionality
- creates a self-service experience
- helps manage tasks, requests, access, bookings, or calculations
- turns a website from presentation into a working tool

##### When it is better not to choose it

Not the best choice if:

- you only need a presentation site
- the business idea is still too vague
- a simpler landing or multi-page website is enough for the current stage
- there is no clear use case for regular user interaction

##### Life scenario example

A business needs a client dashboard, booking interface, calculation tool, internal cabinet, or a lightweight digital service that users interact with regularly in the browser.

##### How it differs from other formats

It is the most functional and interactive format on this list.  
Unlike a regular site, it is built around actions, not only information.  
It is closer to a service than to a presentation page.

### Suggested guidance section: how to choose the right website format

This can be presented as simple recommendation logic on the page:

- If you need a neat online presence and contacts, start with a business-card website.
- If you need to promote one offer and convert traffic, choose a landing page.
- If you have several services and need structure, choose a multi-page website.
- If people need to browse many positions or categories, use a catalog website.
- If trust depends on examples of your work, choose a portfolio website.
- If clients are confused about what fits them, add a website with quiz or diagnostics.
- If the person behind the business is central to trust, choose a personal brand website.
- If people need to use a tool or service, think in terms of a web app or mini-service.

- slightly sharper corners
- stronger contrast than in other directions

#### Icon style

- a mix of line and simple solid accents can work

#### How the homepage can look

The homepage opens with a strong, bold composition:

- powerful heading
- more graphic contrast

- bold section dividers
- distinctive use of negative space

## 21. Homepage wireframe structure

### Role of the homepage

The homepage should work as a guided route, not as a generic portfolio front page.

Its job:

- quickly explain what this site is about
- help the visitor recognize their task
- show possible solution formats
- reduce confusion
- build trust
- lead to diagnostics, cases, or a request

The homepage should feel like:

"I understand what this specialist does, how she thinks, what may fit my situation, and what I should do next."

---

### Section 1. Hero

#### Goal

Immediately explain the positioning and remove confusion around what exactly is offered.

#### Heading

I help you understand which digital solution your business actually needs.

#### Subheading

Websites, bots, applications, AI assistants, and automations are different tools for different tasks.  
I help you choose the right format and build it clearly, calmly, and without unnecessary complexity.

#### Content inside

- main positioning statement
- short explanation of the site's role
- optional trust line about clarity and transparent work
- 2 primary actions
- optional small note that the user can start from task, solution, or diagnostics

#### CTA

- Primary: Choose the right solution
- Secondary: Discuss my task

---

### Section 2. What do you need to solve

#### Goal

Help the visitor start from their situation instead of technical categories.

#### Heading

What do you need to solve?

#### Subheading

If you do not know whether you need a website, bot, application, AI assistant, or automation, start with your task.

#### Content inside

Task cards such as:

- get more requests
- explain services clearly
- automate communication
- simplify booking
- deliver materials automatically
- create a convenient client service
- reduce manual routine
- launch a digital solution without chaos

Each card should include:

- short task title
- 1 to 2 line explanation
- line: what usually fits
- micro-CTA

#### CTA

- On cards: See suitable solution
- Under section: Help me choose by task

---

### Section 3. What I create

#### Goal

Show the main solution formats in a way that feels understandable and structured, not like a dry service list.

#### Heading

What solutions I create

#### Subheading

Different tasks need different formats. Here are the main types of digital solutions I design and build.

#### Content inside

Solution cards:

- websites
- bots
- applications
- AI agents / AI assistants
- automations

Each card should include:

- simple explanation
- when it usually fits
- short difference from the other formats
- link to explore more

#### CTA

- Explore solutions

---

### Section 4. Do not know what to choose

#### Goal

Give a low-pressure path for confused users and convert uncertainty into a guided action.

#### Heading

Not sure what fits you?

#### Subheading

Go through a short diagnostics flow and get a practical recommendation: what suits your task, why, where to start, and what can be added later.

#### Content inside

- short explanation of diagnostics
- note that it takes about 2 minutes
- 3 to 4 short benefits of taking it
- optional mini preview of 1 or 2 example questions

#### CTA

- Take diagnostics

---

### Section 5. Ready starting points

#### Goal

Reduce decision fatigue and offer understandable starter scenarios instead of forcing the visitor to assemble a solution from zero.

#### Heading

How you can start

#### Subheading

If you do not need a big custom system right away, you can begin with a clear starting scenario and expand later.

#### Content inside

Starter solution cards:

- quick start
- for experts
- for booking clients
- for delivering materials
- for automation
- for client support
- for scaling

Each card should include:

- title
- who it is for
- what it includes
- what task it solves
- how it can grow later

#### CTA

- Help me choose a starting point

---

### Section 6. How I work

#### Goal

Show that the work process is thoughtful, structured, and calm.

#### Heading

How I work

#### Subheading

First we understand the task, then choose the right format, and only after that build the solution step by step.

#### Content inside

Step-based process:

- clarify the task
- choose the right starting format
- think through structure and logic
- build the solution
- refine within scope
- launch and explain next steps

Optional support line:

The goal is not to build everything at once, but to create a solution that really fits your stage and task.

#### CTA

- See how the process works

---

### Section 7. Trust and transparency

#### Goal

Reduce anxiety around money, process, scope, and post-launch support.

#### Heading

Transparent work, clear process

#### Subheading

I want you to understand in advance what is included, what is paid separately, how revisions work, and what happens after launch.

#### Content inside

This section can be split into 3 compact sub-blocks:

1. What is included
2. What is usually paid separately
3. What happens after launch

Inside, include short points such as:

- task clarification
- solution logic
- implementation
- launch preparation
- revisions within scope
- domain
- hosting
- external services
- subscriptions
- additional improvements
- support after launch

#### CTA

- Clarify the format of work

---

### Section 8. Cases

#### Goal

Prove competence through real examples and show not just what was built, but why that format was chosen.

#### Heading

Cases and examples

#### Subheading

Not just what was done, but what task was being solved, why this format was chosen, and what result it gave.

#### Content inside

Featured case cards:

- business task
- chosen solution format
- short explanation of the logic
- result

Optional tabs or labels:

- websites
- bots
- applications
- automations
- AI solutions

#### CTA

- View all cases

---

### Section 9. FAQ

#### Goal

Answer typical doubts before the visitor needs to ask them personally.

#### Heading

Frequently asked questions

#### Subheading

Short answers to the questions that usually come up before starting work.

#### Content inside

Questions such as:

- What should I choose: website, bot, application, or automation?
- What if I do not fully understand what I need yet?
- Can I start with a minimal version?
- What is included in the work?
- What is paid separately?
- How long does it usually take?
- What happens after launch?
- Can the project be expanded later?

#### CTA

- Did not find your question? Discuss your task

---

### Section 10. Final CTA

#### Goal

Give the user a calm and clear next step after the page has done its explanatory work.

#### Heading

If you want, we can start with your task and figure out the right format together.

#### Subheading

You do not need to know in advance whether you need a website, bot, application, AI solution, or automation. It is enough to describe the situation, and we can define the best starting point.

#### Content inside

- short reassurance line
- one primary action
- one secondary action

#### CTA

- Primary: Discuss my task
- Secondary: Take diagnostics

---

### Recommended section order summary

1. Hero
2. What do you need to solve
3. What solutions I create
4. Not sure what fits you
5. Ready starting points
6. How I work
7. Trust and transparency
8. Cases
9. FAQ
10. Final CTA

---

### Homepage UX logic

This order works because:

- first the user understands the positioning
- then recognizes their own task
- then sees the available solution types
- then gets help if still unsure
- then sees easy ways to start
- then gains trust through process and transparency
- then sees proof
- then closes remaining questions
- then gets a calm final next step
